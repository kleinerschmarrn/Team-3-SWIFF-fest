Bristol

This year's festival will be held in the vibrant and bustling City of Bristol. Part of the UNESCO Creative Cities Network and designated City of Film, Bristol has a well established reputation as a pioneer and cultivator of media including film production, heritate, development and culture. Amongst other accolades, the city is home to Academy Award®-winning entertainment studio Aardman Animations (est 1972), host of 11 annual international film festivals, houses 11 cinemas and is the birthplace of motion picture pioneer William Friese-Greene (1855-1921) and Hollywood icon Cary Grant (1904-1986). As one of England's most historically rich and culturally vibrant cities, famous for it's independant thinking, creativity and diversity, Bristol provides the perfect location for this year's Southwest Film Festival. Read on to discover more about some of the extraordinary venues where the festival will be taking place...

Lloyds Amphitheatre


A distinctive Bristol harbourside landmark, the Amphitheatre was built by Lloyds bank around Canon House, the former regional headquarters of the bank in Bristol. A striking example of post modernist architecture, the Grade II building it sorrounds draws local inspiration, referencing the crescents of Georgian Bristol and nearby Bath, featuring bold geometric forms and features of classical architecture. Canon House played an important role in the post-industrial regeneration of Bristol’s docks as a thriving recreation and cultural centre. The Ampitheatre continues to maintain this accolade today hosting some of the city's biggest events.

Clifton Observatory


Restored in 2015, the iconic and unique Clifton observatory offers breath-taking views and a unforgettable atmostphere. With a 360 cafe, a museum housing the Camera Obscura and the mythical Giant's Cave with panaromic views of the Avon Gorge, viewings at the Observatory will allow film viewers to immerse them selves in the rich history with an experience to remember.

Queen's Square


Nestled amongst Bristol's harbourside and Old City, the final location is a magnificent and spacious Georgian square lined with trees, sorrounded by cobbled streets and with a history matched by its beauty. A home for the merchant elite of the 18th century and famous for it's role in the violent Bristol riots of 1831, Queen's Square now provides a historic backdrop to a variety of events including outdoor theatre, festivals and concerts.

Brunel's SS Great Britain


Viewings aboard the ship that changed the world. A multi-award winning attraction, the infamous steam ship was rescued from wreckage in 1970 and restored to her Victorian glamour. Highly advanced for her time, she was designed by honorary Bristolian and genius engineer Isambard Kingdom Brunel and was the first Iron steamer to cross the Atlantic. Now at home in Bristol's dry dock, she provides a magical and transportive venue for events today.
